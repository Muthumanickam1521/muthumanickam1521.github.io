### Bridging the Gap Between Brains and Machines

### Introduction
The human brain, with its ~86 billion neurons, is arguably the most complex biological structure in existence. It has inspired one of the most transformative technologies of our time: Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs). But how similar are Biological Neural Networks (BNNs) and ANNs, really?

In this blog, we’ll explore the fascinating parallels and key differences between how the brain and machines learn, adapt, and make predictions — all through the lens of neural networks.

#### 🧩 LLMs and the Brain: How Machines Learn to Predict Like Humans
Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT-4o have revolutionized natural language processing by leveraging a mechanism called self-attention, which enables them to predict sequences of words based on context. But how does this connect with the way your brain functions?

Imagine the sentence:
> “I _ you!”
>Your brain might suggest words like “love,” “miss,” or “hate” depending on context and past experiences. This dynamic, context-aware guessing is similar to how Masked Language Models (MLMs) fill in missing tokens based on patterns and prior knowledge.
In many ways, the human brain behaves like a natural LLM — continuously learning, predicting, and adapting in real-time.

#### 🧠 Key Similarities Between BNNs and ANNs
1. Structure and Components
Neurons vs. Nodes: A single neuron in the brain is analogous to a node in an ANN. Both receive inputs, process them, and produce outputs.
Interconnections: Synapses in the brain are like the weighted connections between nodes in ANNs. These connections determine how information flows through the network.
Weights: Synaptic strength in BNNs is similar to the weights in ANNs. Both determine the influence of one neuron/node on another.
Activation: The firing of a neuron (based on synaptic potential) is akin to the activation function in ANNs, which decides whether a node should “fire” or not.
